Cocoa is a staple food used throughout the world. It has been embraced by different nationalities, cultures, and religions for many years.

It was first developed as a crop by the Aztechs and Mayans thousands of years ago. The Maya Toltec and Aztec people used the bean to create a beverage which they used as a ceremonial drink. The ceremonial drink was created by using sun-feremented bean pods which were roasted then grounded to a paste. Vanilla, occasionally corn, and an assortment of spices were added. The paste was then formed into cake-like structures and were cooled till they hardened. The cakes were mixed with hot water to create the drink xocoatl.

It was exclusively used by the native cultures in these lands until the Spanish Conquistadors arrived on their land and seized the crop after finding its true value. During Christopher Columbus's fourth voyage, he took cocoa beans to Spain. Since then cocoa beans were used in South American cultures and soon spread throughout the world. They utilized the native cocoa to make a drink by mixing in sweeteners and adding spice. As the drink populated across Europe, Europeans believed the drink to be too bitter and added sugar. Through this process, they soon developed chocolate, which soon spread into and across Europe.

In order for cocoa to be used in the assortment of products, the cocoa bean has to be processed. The process cocoa beans undergo is broken up into multiple phases: harvesting, fermentation, cleaning, roasting, grinding, conching, and molding.
